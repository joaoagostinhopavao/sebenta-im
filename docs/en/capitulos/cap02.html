2  Hospital Information Systems and Electronic Health Records

2.1 The Hospital Information System

2.1.1 Definition and components

A Hospital Information System (HIS) is an information system whose data, processes and actors are oriented towards healthcare, integrating clinical, administrative and financial functionalities in a common platform. It is, in essence, the concrete instance of the generic information system model — people, processes, technology — applied to the hospital context.

Three structural components can be distinguished in a HIS:

Data — encompass the clinical and administrative information produced throughout the patient’s journey: patient identification, episodes and diagnoses, procedures performed, images and reports.

Processes — include Admission, Discharge and Transfer (ADT) workflows, operating theatre scheduling and management, billing and clinical coding, and approval workflows with clinical validation.

Technology — comprises databases (relational or NoSQL), standardised integration interfaces (HL7, FHIR), specialist modules (PACS, LIS) and security and access control mechanisms (Haux, 2006).

2.1.2 Modular architecture

The architecture of a HIS is typically modular: a central core — frequently designated the ADT module — aggregates and manages the unique patient record, to which specialist modules are connected by functional area. This architecture ensures that all modules share the same central database, guaranteeing record uniqueness.

The modules that typically constitute a reference HIS are: Emergency/ Admissions, Outpatient Clinic, Inpatient, Operating Theatre, Day Hospital, PACS/Imaging, LIS/Laboratory and Billing/Coding.

2.1.3 The typical patient flow in the HIS

The patient’s journey in a hospital materialises, from an informatics perspective, as a sequence of events recorded in the HIS. The generic flow comprises four key moments: Admission — recording the patient’s entry into the system, whether through the emergency department, an outpatient appointment or a planned admission; Examination — performing and recording diagnostic or therapeutic acts in the specialist modules (laboratory, imaging, theatre); Diagnosis — consolidating the clinical information and formulating the diagnosis; Discharge — closing the episode, with a discharge note and coding for billing.

Note

The ADT (Admission, Discharge, Transfer) module is the operational core of the HIS: it is where the patient’s entry into the system is recorded, bed occupancy is managed and inter-service transfers are handled. All subsequent clinical events are associated with the episode created in the ADT.

2.2 Types of Electronic Health Record

2.2.1 EMR, EHR and PHR — definitions and distinctions

In Medical Informatics vocabulary, three types of electronic health record can be distinguished, each with different scopes and purposes (Garets & Davis, 2006; National Alliance for Health Information Technology, 2008):

The EMR (Electronic Medical Record) is the electronic clinical record of a single organisation. It is created and used by the health professionals and authorised staff of that organisation; it does not follow the patient when they change institution; and it can only be consulted internally. The Portuguese reference example is SClínico, the institutional clinical system integrated within the NHS (SNS) ecosystem.

The EHR (Electronic Health Record) has a multi-institutional scope: it complies with interoperability standards that allow sharing across multiple organisations, follows the patient throughout the entire health system and constitutes the basis of modern health systems. In Portugal, the RSE (Registo de Saúde Electrónico) of the NHS is the reference example.

The PHR (Personal Health Record) is managed, controlled and shared by the patient themselves. It can aggregate data from multiple sources — mobile applications, health sensors, institutional records — and is not necessarily clinical in the strict sense. Platforms such as Apple Health or Google Health are paradigmatic examples. Note that the PHR is complementary to the EHR, not a substitute for it.

2.2.2 From the patient journey to the EHR

The clinical journey of a chronic patient typically involves multiple institutions over time: a hospital admission, follow-up appointments at a health centre, surgery at another hospital, prescriptions dispensed at a pharmacy. Each institution maintains its own EMR, with its own data model, internal identifiers and access rules.

The EHR emerges precisely from the need to aggregate this dispersed information into a longitudinal, shared view of the patient. The interoperability between EMRs is the technical condition that makes the EHR possible — and this is the central challenge that the HL7, FHIR standards and IHE profiles seek to address (Benson & Grieve, 2021).

2.2.3 Technical requirements of the EHR

For an EHR to be operational, three fundamental technical requirements must be met that are mutually dependent:

  • Patient uniqueness — a unique and universal identifier must exist to unambiguously associate all dispersed records with the same patient. In its absence, the EHR becomes impossible and clinical errors due to identity confusion become likely (Just et al., 2016).

  • Technical interoperability between systems — institutional systems must be able to communicate with each other, using standardised protocols and formats (HL7 v2, FHIR R4).

  • Standardised terminologies — for data to have the same meaning between systems, shared controlled vocabularies must be used (SNOMED CT for clinical concepts, LOINC for laboratory observations, ICD for diagnoses).

2.3 SONHO — Portuguese Case Study

2.3.1 Characterisation and role in the NHS

SONHO (Sistema de Informação Hospitalar) is the reference HIS of the Portuguese National Health Service, present in approximately 90% of public hospitals. In continuous production since the 1990s, it is functionally classified as an EMR: its scope is institutional, and its interoperability with external systems is limited by the original architecture.

SONHO’s role in the NHS is organised around five essential functions: unique patient identification by NHS number; administrative management of the hospital episode; recording of emergency, inpatient, outpatient and theatre activity; discharge recording and DRG-based billing coding; and serving as an integration backbone for the hospital’s other systems (SPMS – Serviços Partilhados do Ministério da Saúde, 2019).

2.3.3 Limitations and evolution trajectory

SONHO has structural limitations arising from its original architecture, developed at a time when current interoperability requirements did not exist:

Obsolete architecture — the original version runs on a Progress database, 1990s technology, with a monolithic architecture subject to availability failures and serious difficulty integrating with modern HL7 and FHIR standards.

Licensing costs — migration to Oracle RDBMS in the most recent version resolved some performance issues but introduced vendor lock-in with high per-hospital licensing costs and slow updates.

The evolution trajectory points to three vectors: SClínico as the evolving clinical platform; RSE as the NHS EHR component; and FHIR integration planned in national roadmaps, driven by European regulatory pressure from the EHDS — European Health Data Space (Parlamento Europeu e Conselho da União Europeia, 2025).

2.4 Patient Identification

2.4.1 The problem of record uniqueness

Unambiguous patient identification is an absolute prerequisite for any EHR system. The fundamental problem arises from the fact that the same patient may have different identifiers in each hospital, health centre or system they visit. Without uniqueness, the EHR is technically impossible and clinical errors due to identity confusion become structurally likely (Just et al., 2016).

This is not merely an administrative problem: the incorrect association of records — for example, confusing two patients with similar names — can have serious clinical consequences, including medication errors, incompatible transfusions or surgery on the wrong patient.

The National Patient Number (NNU), incorporated in the NHS number, is the reference identifier in all NHS information systems. Its assignment is unique and permanent, guaranteeing longitudinal traceability of the patient throughout their entire life within the health system.

2.4.3 Master Patient Index and the RNU

The concept of Master Patient Index (MPI) designates the centralised system for matching each institution’s local identifiers with the patient’s universal identifier. In Portugal, this function is performed by the RNU (Registo Nacional de Utentes), managed by SPMS (Serviços Partilhados do Ministério da Saúde).

The RNU works as follows: each hospital maintains its own local MRNs; when a patient is admitted, the system queries the RNU to obtain the corresponding NNU; the matching is performed by combining name, date of birth, tax ID and NNU.

2.5 Continuity of Care

2.5.1 Definition and dimensions

Continuity of care is defined as the ability of a health professional to access the relevant clinical history of a patient, regardless of when or where care was provided (Haggerty et al., 2003). It is a multidimensional concept that the literature organises into three components:

Informational continuity — the clinical record follows the patient. Diagnoses, active medication, allergies and previous episodes are available at the point of care, regardless of the institution. This dimension is most directly dependent on the existence of an operational EHR.

Management continuity — treatment plans and previous clinical decisions are known and respected by the professionals providing care at each moment. It avoids duplicate examinations and contradictory prescriptions, with a direct impact on safety and efficiency.

Relational continuity — the patient has a GP or reference team who knows their clinical history longitudinally. The EHR facilitates communication and coordination between the different providers who make up this extended team.

2.5.2 The EHR as a technical requirement for continuity

Continuity of care is not, therefore, merely a clinical aspiration: it has precise technical requirements that the EHR must satisfy. These requirements are articulated at three mutually dependent levels:

  1. Patient uniqueness via MPI (RNU in Portugal) — without this condition, it is not possible to aggregate the records of the same patient from different institutions.

  2. Technical interoperability via HL7 and FHIR — without this condition, systems cannot exchange data in a structured and automatic way.

  3. Standardised terminologies (SNOMED CT, LOINC, ICD) — without this condition, data reach the destination but are not correctly interpreted, compromising semantic interoperability.

Tip

Summary: technical interoperability is necessary but not sufficient for continuity of care. For different systems to understand each other, data must share the same meaning — which requires standardised terminologies. It is this articulation between the three levels that makes the EHR effective in clinical practice.

2.6 Summary

This chapter addressed the architecture of hospital information systems and the different types of electronic health record. SONHO was analysed as a case study of the Portuguese health system, highlighting both its capabilities and its structural limitations. Patient identification and continuity of care were framed as technical and clinical requirements that converge in the concept of the EHR.

The following key points are retained:

  1. The HIS is the concrete instance of the information system in the hospital context, with modular architecture centred on the ADT core.
  2. EMR, EHR and PHR differ in scope and interoperability: the EHR follows the patient; the EMR stays in the institution.
  3. Without patient uniqueness (MPI/RNU), it is not possible to build an effective EHR — identification is a requirement, not a detail.
  4. Continuity of care depends simultaneously on technical and semantic interoperability; neither is sufficient alone.

pressure from the EHDS — *European Health Data Space* [@EHDS2025].

## Patient Identification {#sec-identification}

### The problem of record uniqueness

Unambiguous patient identification is an absolute prerequisite for
any EHR system. The fundamental problem arises from the fact that the
same patient may have different identifiers in each hospital, health
centre or system they visit. Without uniqueness, the EHR is technically
impossible and clinical errors due to identity confusion become
structurally likely [@Just2016].

This is not merely an administrative problem: the incorrect association
of records — for example, confusing two patients with similar names —
can have serious clinical consequences, including medication errors,
incompatible transfusions or surgery on the wrong patient.

### Patient identifiers in Portugal

Four identifiers relevant to patient identification coexist in the
Portuguese health system:

| Identifier | Description |
|---|---|
| **NHS No.** | NHS patient number — 9 digits, unique nationwide, permanent |
| **Citizen's Card** | Civil identification — used at admission to confirm identity |
| **MRN** | *Medical Record Number* — internal number of each institution |
| **Tax ID** | Tax Identification Number — used for billing |

: Patient identifiers in Portugal {#tbl-identifiers}

The **National Patient Number (NNU)**, incorporated in the NHS number,
is the reference identifier in all NHS information systems. Its
assignment is unique and permanent, guaranteeing longitudinal
traceability of the patient throughout their entire life within the
health system.

### Master Patient Index and the RNU

The concept of *Master Patient Index* (MPI) designates the centralised
system for matching each institution's local identifiers with the
patient's universal identifier. In Portugal, this function is performed
by the **RNU** (*Registo Nacional de Utentes*), managed by SPMS
(*Serviços Partilhados do Ministério da Saúde*).

The RNU works as follows: each hospital maintains its own local MRNs;
when a patient is admitted, the system queries the RNU to obtain the
corresponding NNU; the matching is performed by combining name, date of
birth, tax ID and NNU.

## Continuity of Care {#sec-continuity}

### Definition and dimensions

Continuity of care is defined as the ability of a health professional
to access the relevant clinical history of a patient, regardless of
when or where care was provided [@Haggerty2003]. It is a
multidimensional concept that the literature organises into three
components:

**Informational continuity** — the clinical record follows the patient.
Diagnoses, active medication, allergies and previous episodes are
available at the point of care, regardless of the institution. This
dimension is most directly dependent on the existence of an operational
EHR.

**Management continuity** — treatment plans and previous clinical
decisions are known and respected by the professionals providing care
at each moment. It avoids duplicate examinations and contradictory
prescriptions, with a direct impact on safety and efficiency.

**Relational continuity** — the patient has a GP or reference team who
knows their clinical history longitudinally. The EHR facilitates
communication and coordination between the different providers who make
up this extended team.

### The EHR as a technical requirement for continuity

Continuity of care is not, therefore, merely a clinical aspiration:
it has precise technical requirements that the EHR must satisfy. These
requirements are articulated at three mutually dependent levels:

1. **Patient uniqueness** via MPI (RNU in Portugal) — without this
   condition, it is not possible to aggregate the records of the same
   patient from different institutions.

2. **Technical interoperability** via HL7 and FHIR — without this
   condition, systems cannot exchange data in a structured and
   automatic way.

3. **Standardised terminologies** (SNOMED CT, LOINC, ICD) — without
   this condition, data reach the destination but are not correctly
   interpreted, compromising semantic interoperability.

::: {.callout-tip}
**Summary:** technical interoperability is necessary but not sufficient
for continuity of care. For different systems to understand each other,
data must share the same meaning — which requires standardised
terminologies. It is this articulation between the three levels that
makes the EHR effective in clinical practice.
:::

## Summary {#sec-summary-02}

This chapter addressed the architecture of hospital information systems
and the different types of electronic health record. SONHO was analysed
as a case study of the Portuguese health system, highlighting both its
capabilities and its structural limitations. Patient identification and
continuity of care were framed as technical and clinical requirements
that converge in the concept of the EHR.

The following key points are retained:

1. The HIS is the concrete instance of the information system in the
   hospital context, with modular architecture centred on the ADT core.
2. EMR, EHR and PHR differ in scope and interoperability: the EHR
   follows the patient; the EMR stays in the institution.
3. Without patient uniqueness (MPI/RNU), it is not possible to build
   an effective EHR — identification is a requirement, not a detail.
4. Continuity of care depends simultaneously on technical and semantic
   interoperability; neither is sufficient alone.
