1  Foundations of Medical Informatics

1.1 What is Medical Informatics

1.1.1 Definition and disciplinary scope

Medical Informatics is a scientific discipline at the intersection of three knowledge domains: Information Science, Computer Science and Health Science (Haux, 2006). Its object of study focuses on how information is generated, organised, stored, retrieved and used in the clinical and organisational context of healthcare.

There is a frequent tendency to reduce Medical Informatics to the mere application of computing tools in healthcare. This perspective is, however, reductive: it is a discipline that integrates technology, information management and clinical practice in an inseparable way, requiring simultaneous competence in all three constituent domains (Collen & Ball, 2015).

1.1.2 The three foundational pillars

Three structural pillars underpin the discipline:

Information — encompasses models for representing clinical knowledge, data semantics and its formal structuring. The quality of clinical information is decisive for patient safety and continuity of care.

Computing — comprises information systems, network architectures and computational models that support the processing and communication of clinical data. This pillar provides the technical means without which information sharing between systems would be impossible.

Health — encompasses clinical practice, care processes and the ethical and legal responsibilities arising from the use of information systems in the context of patient care. No technological solution can be designed without a solid understanding of the clinical domain it aims to support.

1.1.3 Differences from conventional informatics

Medical Informatics differs from general-purpose informatics through a set of characteristics that impose additional requirements for rigour, safety and accountability:

  • Sensitive and critical data — clinical data are, by nature, special-category personal data subject to specific legal frameworks (notably the General Data Protection Regulation and sectoral health legislation).

  • Direct clinical impact — errors in recording, transmitting or interpreting clinical information can have direct consequences for patient health and safety.

  • Demanding regulation — health information systems are subject to technical standards and legal requirements with no parallel in other sectors.

  • High organisational complexity — hospitals and health centres are highly complex, multidisciplinary, multi-service organisations, which poses particular challenges for the integration and governance of information systems.

1.2 Historical Evolution

1.2.1 From hospital administration to digital health

The history of Medical Informatics can be traced across four major periods, each marked by a technological transformation that profoundly altered how clinical information is managed.

1960s–70s: first hospital administrative systems. The first computing systems introduced in hospitals were essentially administrative in scope: admission management, bed control and billing processing. The clinical record remained, almost entirely, on paper.

1980s–90s: clinical computerisation and digital imaging. This period was marked by the progressive introduction of systems supporting clinical activity and, particularly significantly, by the development of the DICOM standard (Digital Imaging and Communications in Medicine), which normalised the storage and transmission of digital medical images, establishing the technological foundation for modern PACS systems.

2000s: HL7 integration and electronic records. The first decade of the century saw the widespread adoption of electronic clinical records and the adoption of the HL7 (Health Level Seven) standard as the integration language between hospital systems. Technical interoperability between different applications became an operational requirement, not merely an aspiration.

Today: FHIR APIs, interoperability and digital health. The current paradigm rests on the FHIR standard (Fast Healthcare Interoperability Resources), which replaces the classical HL7 messaging model with a REST API architecture, making integration more agile and aligned with modern web standards. There is simultaneously growing pressure to create European-scale health data spaces, materialised in the EHDS regulation (Parlamento Europeu e Conselho da União Europeia, 2025).

1.3 The Hospital as a Sociotechnical System

1.3.1 Technology, people and process dimensions

The modern hospital is not simply a healthcare organisation — it is a complex sociotechnical system in which technology, people and processes articulate in an inseparable way. No information system creates value if it is designed or implemented in isolation, without considering the other two vertices of this triangle.

The technological dimension encompasses the information systems themselves, the network infrastructure that connects them and the medical devices that generate and consume clinical data. Technology is a necessary but not sufficient condition for improving healthcare.

The people dimension comprises health professionals (physicians, nurses, technicians), hospital managers and administrators, and the patients and their carers. People produce, validate and use clinical information; they also bear the consequences of any failures in the systems.

The process dimension encompasses the clinical workflows that structure care activity, protocols and best-practice guidelines, and the inter-service coordination mechanisms. The quality of processes is frequently the determining factor for the success or failure of a hospital computerisation project.

1.3.2 Hospital complexity

The particular complexity of hospitals results from three structural characteristics:

  • Multidisciplinary nature — different clinical specialties, each with their own language, processes and organisational culture, coexist within the same institution and share information about the same patients.

  • Multi-service nature — each hospital service holds a significant degree of functional and organisational autonomy, which complicates the adoption of uniform solutions and centralised information governance.

  • Multiplicity of systems — a medium-sized hospital typically runs dozens of specialist information systems (central hospital system, medical imaging system, laboratory system, operating theatre system, among others), each with its own data models, interfaces and vendors.

1.4 Health Information Systems

1.4.1 Definition of an information system

An information system is defined as an organised set of people, processes and technology that collects, stores, processes and distributes information with the objective of supporting decision-making in an organisation (Haux, 2006). This tripartite definition — input, processing, output — applies both to generic information systems and to systems specifically designed for the healthcare context.

In the hospital domain, the input corresponds to the collection of clinical and administrative data generated throughout the patient’s journey; processing involves storing, validating and structuring those data; and the output materialises as information available to support clinical, managerial and statistical decisions.

1.4.2 The data–information–knowledge chain

Three levels can be distinguished in the hierarchy of clinical information, with direct implications for the design and evaluation of systems:

Data are isolated records without context: a numeric value, a date, a code. On their own, data have no operational clinical meaning.

Information results from contextualising data: a numeric value associated with a physiological parameter, a patient and a point in time becomes information with clinical utility.

Knowledge emerges when information is interpreted in the light of clinical models and accumulated experience, enabling a diagnostic or therapeutic decision to be justified.

Medical Informatics is not merely concerned with storing data, but with transforming them into knowledge useful for clinical practice.

1.4.3 Organisational flows and the structural role of IS

Clinical information does not exist in isolated silos: it circulates continuously between services, between professionals and between institutions. From this circulation depends the coordination between the different actors in the health system; from coordination depends continuity of care; and from continuity of care depends, ultimately, patient safety.

This establishes a logical chain with direct implications for the governance of hospital information systems:

No information systems → no coordination → no continuity of care → increased clinical risk.

This chain demonstrates that information systems are not, in modern hospitals, a technological convenience: they are critical infrastructure. Their unavailability or degradation has direct effects on the quality and safety of care provided.

1.5 Interoperability

1.5.1 Three levels of interoperability

Interoperability is the ability of different systems to exchange information and to use that information effectively (Benson & Grieve, 2021). In the healthcare context, three complementary levels can be identified:

Technical interoperability — systems can communicate at the level of transport, format and protocol. It is a necessary but not sufficient condition: it guarantees that data reach the destination, not that they are correctly interpreted.

Semantic interoperability — systems share the meaning of the data exchanged. For two systems to “understand each other”, they must use controlled vocabularies and standardised terminologies (such as SNOMED CT, LOINC or ICD), so that the same clinical concept has the same meaning in both systems.

Organisational interoperability — processes, policies and the legal framework are compatible between organisations wishing to share information. This level is frequently the most difficult to achieve, as it requires governance agreements that go beyond the purely technical dimension.

Illustrative example

Two hospitals can exchange messages (technical interoperability). One records the diagnosis as “Arterial hypertension”; the other uses the abbreviation “HTN”. The systems communicate — but do they understand each other? No, in the absence of shared standardised terminologies. This example illustrates the difference between technical and semantic interoperability.

1.5.2 The problem of information fragmentation

One of the main obstacles to interoperability in current health systems is the fragmentation of clinical information: the same patient may have records distributed across multiple systems, in multiple institutions, without any effective aggregation and sharing mechanism.

The practical consequences of this fragmentation are well documented (Haggerty et al., 2003):

  • unnecessary repetition of examinations due to unavailability of previous results;
  • delays in clinical decision-making due to unavailability of relevant information at the point of care;
  • increased clinical risk associated with medication errors, unrecorded allergies or contradictory diagnoses across institutions.

1.5.3 Current challenges

The main challenges facing Medical Informatics today can be grouped into four vectors:

  • Fragmentation — clinical information remains dispersed across heterogeneous systems, without effective interoperability between them.

  • Legacy systems — many hospitals operate with systems installed decades ago, with monolithic architectures and limited capacity to integrate with modern technologies.

  • Security and data protection — the increasing digitalisation of clinical data expands the attack surface and requires continuous investment in cybersecurity and regulatory compliance.

  • Technological sustainability — the accelerating evolution of information technologies imposes update cycles that health organisations, with limited resources, struggle to keep pace with.

1.6 Summary

This chapter established the conceptual foundations on which Medical Informatics rests as a scientific discipline. The concepts introduced — sociotechnical system, data–information–knowledge chain, levels of interoperability — will be revisited and deepened as technical standards and integration architectures are introduced.

The following key points are retained:

  1. Medical Informatics is an intersection discipline that cannot be reduced to any one of its three constituent domains in isolation.
  2. The hospital is a sociotechnical system: technology only produces value when aligned with clinical processes and organisational practices.
  3. Interoperability has three levels — technical, semantic and organisational — and the absence of any one of them compromises continuity of care.
  4. The fragmentation of clinical information is the main obstacle to health system integration and has direct consequences for patient safety.

## Health Information Systems {#sec-his-general}

processes and technology that collects, stores, processes and
distributes information with the objective of supporting decision-making

### Organisational flows and the structural role of IS

Clinical information does not exist in isolated silos: it circulates
continuously between services, between professionals and between
institutions. From this circulation depends the coordination between
the different actors in the health system; from coordination depends
continuity of care; and from continuity of care depends, ultimately,
patient safety.

This establishes a logical chain with direct implications for the
governance of hospital information systems:

> *No information systems → no coordination → no continuity of care →
> increased clinical risk.*

This chain demonstrates that information systems are not, in modern
hospitals, a technological convenience: they are critical infrastructure.
Their unavailability or degradation has direct effects on the quality
and safety of care provided.

## Interoperability {#sec-interop}

### Three levels of interoperability

Interoperability is the ability of different systems to exchange
information and to use that information effectively [@BensonGrieve2021].
In the healthcare context, three complementary levels can be identified:

**Technical interoperability** — systems can communicate at the level
of transport, format and protocol. It is a necessary but not sufficient
condition: it guarantees that data reach the destination, not that they
are correctly interpreted.

**Semantic interoperability** — systems share the meaning of the data
exchanged. For two systems to "understand each other", they must use
controlled vocabularies and standardised terminologies (such as
SNOMED CT, LOINC or ICD), so that the same clinical concept has the
same meaning in both systems.

**Organisational interoperability** — processes, policies and the
legal framework are compatible between organisations wishing to share
information. This level is frequently the most difficult to achieve,
as it requires governance agreements that go beyond the purely technical
dimension.

**Illustrative example**

Two hospitals can exchange messages (technical interoperability).
One records the diagnosis as "Arterial hypertension"; the other uses
the abbreviation "HTN". The systems communicate — but do they
understand each other? No, in the absence of shared standardised
terminologies. This example illustrates the difference between
technical and semantic interoperability.
:::

### The problem of information fragmentation

One of the main obstacles to interoperability in current health
systems is the fragmentation of clinical information: the same patient
may have records distributed across multiple systems, in multiple
institutions, without any effective aggregation and sharing mechanism.

The practical consequences of this fragmentation are well documented
[@Haggerty2003]:

- unnecessary repetition of examinations due to unavailability of
  previous results;
- delays in clinical decision-making due to unavailability of relevant
  information at the point of care;
- increased clinical risk associated with medication errors, unrecorded
  allergies or contradictory diagnoses across institutions.

### Current challenges

The main challenges facing Medical Informatics today can be grouped
into four vectors:

- **Fragmentation** — clinical information remains dispersed across
  heterogeneous systems, without effective interoperability between
  them.

- **Legacy systems** — many hospitals operate with systems installed
  decades ago, with monolithic architectures and limited capacity to
  integrate with modern technologies.

- **Security and data protection** — the increasing digitalisation of
  clinical data expands the attack surface and requires continuous
  investment in cybersecurity and regulatory compliance.

- **Technological sustainability** — the accelerating evolution of
  information technologies imposes update cycles that health
  organisations, with limited resources, struggle to keep pace with.

## Summary {#sec-summary-01}

This chapter established the conceptual foundations on which Medical
Informatics rests as a scientific discipline. The concepts introduced —
sociotechnical system, data–information–knowledge chain, levels of
interoperability — will be revisited and deepened as technical standards
and integration architectures are introduced.

The following key points are retained:

1. Medical Informatics is an intersection discipline that cannot be
   reduced to any one of its three constituent domains in isolation.
2. The hospital is a sociotechnical system: technology only produces
   value when aligned with clinical processes and organisational
   practices.
3. Interoperability has three levels — technical, semantic and
   organisational — and the absence of any one of them compromises
   continuity of care.
4. The fragmentation of clinical information is the main obstacle to
   health system integration and has direct consequences for patient
   safety.
