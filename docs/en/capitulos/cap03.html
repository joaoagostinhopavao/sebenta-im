3  Terminologies and Semantic Interoperability

3.1 The Problem of Free Text

3.1.1 Ambiguity in clinical information

Free-text clinical documentation is, historically, the dominant form of record-keeping in health systems. However, free text introduces a structural problem: semantic ambiguity. The same clinical concept can be expressed in different ways by different professionals, in different contexts, making automatic aggregation and comparison of information impossible (Benson & Grieve, 2021).

A paradigmatic example: the term “high tension” can be interpreted as systemic arterial hypertension, emotional tension or tension headache, depending on the professional and the clinical context. Three distinct interpretations of the same free-text term, with completely different diagnostic and therapeutic implications.

The practical consequences of this ambiguity are well documented: communication errors between professionals; inability to aggregate data for statistics or clinical research; failure in continuity of care between institutions; and, ultimately, systems that exchange data but do not share meaning.

3.1.2 From ambiguity to structured coding

The solution to the ambiguity problem lies in replacing free text with structured coding: the assignment of standardised codes to clinical concepts, so that the same concept always has the same code, regardless of who records it or where it is recorded. Figure 3.1 illustrates this contrast.

"Patient with high blood pressure, elevated sugar, complains of chest pain since yesterday."

3.2 Key Concepts

3.2.1 Technical versus semantic interoperability

The distinction between technical and semantic interoperability is central to Medical Informatics and must be clarified precisely:

Technical interoperability refers to the ability of two systems to exchange data at the level of transport, format and protocol. It guarantees that bytes reach the destination; it does not guarantee that meaning is preserved. Example: sending an HL7 v2 message between two systems.

Semantic interoperability refers to the ability of two systems to share the meaning of the data exchanged. It guarantees that the receiver interprets the message content in the same way as the sender. Example: both systems interpret code 386661006 as fever, because both use SNOMED CT (Benson & Grieve, 2021).

3.2.2 Terminology, classification and ontology

Three related but distinct concepts structure the clinical coding ecosystem:

A terminology is a controlled, detailed vocabulary that codes clinical data entry. It admits multiple synonyms per concept and aims for granular clinical documentation. SNOMED CT is the reference example.

A classification groups concepts into fixed categories for statistical output and reporting purposes. Each concept occupies a unique place in the hierarchical tree. ICD and ICF are the paradigmatic examples.

An ontology is a formal knowledge model that defines concepts and their logical relationships, enabling automatic inference. SNOMED CT is simultaneously a terminology and an ontology: its relationship structure enables computational reasoning over concepts.

3.3 SNOMED CT

3.3.1 What is SNOMED CT?

SNOMED CT (Systematized Nomenclature of Medicine — Clinical Terms) is the most comprehensive clinical terminology and ontology system in the world. Maintained by SNOMED International, it is in use in more than 40 countries and constitutes the reference terminology for coding clinical data entry in modern EHRs (SNOMED International, 2024).

Its scope is organised across three dimensions: terminology (controlled clinical vocabulary with more than 350,000 concepts), interoperability (standardisation of clinical language between institutions and systems) and ontological structure (poly-hierarchical graph of concepts and relationships).

3.3.2 The three fundamental components

SNOMED CT is organised around three components that work together to represent clinical knowledge precisely and computably:

Concepts — atomic units of clinical meaning, each identified by a unique and permanent numeric Concept ID. Examples: 38341003 = Hypertension; 73211009 = Diabetes mellitus; 386661006 = Fever.

Descriptions — human-readable terms associated with each concept: the Fully Specified Name (FSN), which specifies the concept unambiguously; the Preferred Term, the preferred name in the local language; and synonyms, allowing multiple designations for the same concept.

Relationships — typed links between concepts that form the ontological graph. The main types are: is-a (taxonomic hierarchy), finding-site (anatomical location), and causative-agent (causative agent). The poly-hierarchy allows a concept to have multiple parents, reflecting the complexity of clinical reality.

3.3.3 Example: Bacterial Pneumonia

The concept of bacterial pneumonia illustrates the expressive richness of SNOMED CT through its relationships. The formal expression of the concept is:

53084003 | Bacterial pneumonia (disorder)
├─ finding-site: 39607008 | Lung structure
└─ causative-agent: 56415008 | Genus Streptococcus

Compact form (ECL syntax — Expression Constraint Language):

53084003:363698007=39607008,246075003=9861002

This example demonstrates that SNOMED CT does not merely name concepts: it expresses complex clinical relationships in a computable form, enabling automatic reasoning over medical knowledge. Figure 3.2 represents this hierarchy graphically.

3.4 ICD — International Classification of Diseases

3.4.1 Characterisation and purpose

The ICD (International Classification of Diseases and Related Health Problems) is a classification system maintained by the World Health Organisation (WHO) and adopted by 194 member states. The version currently in force is ICD-11, adopted in 2022, with more than 55,000 codes organised across 26 chapters (World Health Organization, 2022).

The purpose of the ICD is fundamentally distinct from SNOMED CT: while the latter serves clinical data entry with high granularity, the ICD serves output — epidemiological reporting, health statistics and hospital billing. It is a classification, not a terminology: it organises diseases into fixed categories according to a mono-axial hierarchy.

3.4.2 Hierarchical structure

The ICD structure is organised at three levels: chapter, block and category. The example of the respiratory chapter illustrates this organisation:

└── Block CA00–CA08 — Acute respiratory infections
├── CA40 — Common cold
├── CA40.0 — Acute rhinitis
└── CA40.Z — Cold, unspecified

3.4.3 SNOMED CT versus ICD — complementarity

SNOMED CT and ICD are not competitors: they are complementary, each suited to its purpose. SNOMED CT encodes clinical detail at the point of care; ICD classifies for reporting and billing. SNOMED International maintains official maps between the two systems, allowing a SNOMED CT concept to be automatically translated into the corresponding ICD code.

3.5 LOINC — Laboratory Observations

3.5.1 Definition and scope

LOINC (Logical Observation Identifiers Names and Codes) is a terminology maintained by the Regenstrief Institute that provides a universal language for identifying laboratory measurements, clinical observations and health documents (McDonald et al., 2003). Its scope is organised across three domains: clinical laboratory (full blood count, glucose, tumour markers); physiological observations (vital signs, assessment scales); and documents (discharge notes, reports).

3.5.2 The anatomy of a LOINC code — the six axes

Each LOINC code is an opaque identifier (e.g. 2345-7), whose meaning is entirely encoded in its formal name. This name results from the combination of six axes that describe, precisely, all relevant aspects of a measurement:

Code 2345-7 corresponds to: Glucose [Mass/volume] in Serum or Plasma. This unique identifier ensures that two laboratories from different institutions report blood glucose in a comparable way, regardless of their internal systems. Figure 3.3 visualises the six axes for this concrete example.

3.6 ICF — International Classification of Functioning

The ICF (International Classification of Functioning, Disability and Health) is a WHO classification that complements the ICD, shifting the focus from disease to functioning and social participation (World Health Organization, 2001). While the ICD answers the question “what does the patient have?”, the ICF answers “what can the patient do?”.

This distinction is clinically relevant: two patients with the same ICD diagnosis may have completely different functioning profiles, with different implications for rehabilitation, social support and return to activity.

The ICF adopts a biopsychosocial model, recognising that disability results from the interaction between the individual’s health condition and contextual factors — both personal and environmental.

3.7 Integration of Terminologies in the Clinical Workflow

None of the terminologies described operates in isolation. Effective semantic interoperability results from their coordinated use throughout the clinical workflow:

At the time of clinical recording, the health professional codes symptoms, diagnoses and procedures in SNOMED CT, ensuring the semantic precision of data entry.

At the time of the laboratory examination, requests and results are identified by LOINC codes, ensuring that the same analysis has the same meaning in any laboratory.

At the time of reporting and billing, SNOMED CT codes are automatically mapped to ICD, through the official maps maintained by SNOMED International, producing the statistical and billing data required by health authorities. Figure 3.4 summarises this integrated workflow.

3.8 Summary

This chapter addressed the problem of semantic ambiguity in clinical information and the standardised terminologies that resolve it. The SNOMED CT / LOINC / ICD / ICF ecosystem was presented as a coordinated set of tools, each with a distinct purpose in the clinical workflow, and none sufficient on its own.

The following key points are retained:

  1. Free text is the main obstacle to semantic interoperability; structured coding is its operational alternative.
  2. Semantic interoperability is distinct from technical: it is not enough for data to reach the destination — they must be correctly interpreted.
  3. SNOMED CT is the reference clinical entry terminology; ICD is the output classification for reporting and billing; LOINC standardises laboratory observations; ICF addresses the functioning dimension.
  4. The terminologies are complementary and linked through official maps: the clinician records in SNOMED CT; the system maps to ICD; the manager obtains comparable statistics.

### SNOMED CT versus ICD — complementarity

SNOMED CT and ICD are not competitors: they are complementary, each
suited to its purpose. SNOMED CT encodes clinical detail at the point
of care; ICD classifies for reporting and billing. SNOMED International
maintains official maps between the two systems, allowing a SNOMED CT
concept to be automatically translated into the corresponding ICD code.

| Dimension | SNOMED CT | ICD |
|---|---|---|
| **Type** | Clinical terminology | Statistical classification |
| **Function** | Input — clinical recording | Output — reporting and billing |
| **Granularity** | Very high (>350,000) | Medium (~55,000) |
| **Structure** | Poly-hierarchical graph | Mono-hierarchical tree |
| **Maintained by** | SNOMED International | WHO |

## LOINC — Laboratory Observations {#sec-loinc}

### Definition and scope

LOINC (*Logical Observation Identifiers Names and Codes*) is a
terminology maintained by the Regenstrief Institute that provides a
universal language for identifying laboratory measurements, clinical
observations and health documents [@McDonald2003]. Its scope is
organised across three domains: clinical laboratory (full blood count,
glucose, tumour markers); physiological observations (vital signs,
assessment scales); and documents (discharge notes, reports).

### The anatomy of a LOINC code — the six axes

Each LOINC code is an opaque identifier (e.g. `2345-7`), whose meaning
is entirely encoded in its formal name. This name results from the
combination of six axes that describe, precisely, all relevant aspects
of a measurement:

| Axis | Name | Question | Example (blood glucose) |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Component | What? | Glucose |
| 2 | Property | Which quantity? | MCnc (mass/volume) |
| 3 | Timing | When? | Pt (point in time) |
| 4 | System | Where? | Ser/Plas (serum/plasma) |
| 5 | Scale | How? | Qn (quantitative) |
| 6 | Method | With what? | (optional) Enzymatic |

## ICF — International Classification of Functioning {#sec-icf}

The ICF (*International Classification of Functioning, Disability and
Health*) is a WHO classification that complements the ICD, shifting
the focus from disease to **functioning** and social participation
[@WHO_ICF]. While the ICD answers the question *"what does the patient
have?"*, the ICF answers *"what can the patient do?"*.

This distinction is clinically relevant: two patients with the same
ICD diagnosis may have completely different functioning profiles, with
different implications for rehabilitation, social support and return
to activity.

The ICF adopts a biopsychosocial model, recognising that disability
results from the interaction between the individual's health condition
and contextual factors — both personal and environmental.

## Integration of Terminologies in the Clinical Workflow {#sec-integration}

None of the terminologies described operates in isolation. Effective
semantic interoperability results from their coordinated use throughout
the clinical workflow:

At the time of **clinical recording**, the health professional codes
symptoms, diagnoses and procedures in SNOMED CT, ensuring the semantic
precision of data entry.

At the time of the **laboratory examination**, requests and results are
identified by LOINC codes, ensuring that the same analysis has the same
meaning in any laboratory.

At the time of **reporting and billing**, SNOMED CT codes are
automatically mapped to ICD, through the official maps maintained by
SNOMED International, producing the statistical and billing data
required by health authorities. Figure 3.4 summarises this integrated
workflow.

## Summary {#sec-summary-03}

This chapter addressed the problem of semantic ambiguity in clinical
information and the standardised terminologies that resolve it. The
SNOMED CT / LOINC / ICD / ICF ecosystem was presented as a coordinated
set of tools, each with a distinct purpose in the clinical workflow,
and none sufficient on its own.

The following key points are retained:

1. Free text is the main obstacle to semantic interoperability;
   structured coding is its operational alternative.
2. Semantic interoperability is distinct from technical: it is not
   enough for data to reach the destination — they must be correctly
   interpreted.
3. SNOMED CT is the reference clinical entry terminology; ICD is the
   output classification for reporting and billing; LOINC standardises
   laboratory observations; ICF addresses the functioning dimension.
4. The terminologies are complementary and linked through official
   maps: the clinician records in SNOMED CT; the system maps to ICD;
   the manager obtains comparable statistics.
